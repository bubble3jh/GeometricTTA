## Additional Hypothesis Testing (Decisive Minimum Set)

The following hypotheses are designed to act as "decisive tests." Passing or failing these will immediately narrow down the exact design space for the subsequent method. Each entry specifies the hypothesis, measurement method, pass/fail criteria, and interpretation.

---

### H23: Disentangling $R^2=1.0$ — Meaningful Confusion Mixing vs. Trivial Low-Dimensional Collapse

**Background Problem:**
If both $v_{true}$ and $v_{pseudo}$ consist of 10 prototypes ($10 \times 512$), they are both rank $\le 10$. Furthermore, if $d_{eff}$ is extremely low (1~8), it becomes structurally trivial for them to fall into each other's span. Thus, the previous result of $R^2=1.0$ might not be strong evidence for "assignment confusion," but rather an artifact of low-dimensional representation where any linear combination is easily achievable.

**Hypotheses (H23):**
* **H23a:** $v_{pseudo}$ is not just any linear combination of $v_{true}$. The coefficient matrix $A$ represents a valid probabilistic mixture (non-negative, row sum = 1) and aligns strictly with the empirical confusion matrix.
* **H23b:** $R^2=1.0$ is merely a consequence of the low-dimensional span. The matrix $A$ cannot be interpreted as a meaningful probabilistic mixture.

**Measurement:**
Replace the standard regression with a **Constrained Regression**:
$$\min_A \|V_{pseudo} - A V_{true}\|_F^2 \quad \text{s.t. } A \ge 0, \; A\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$$
1.  Compute the distance (e.g., $\|A - \hat{A}\|_F$ or row-wise KL divergence) between this fitted $A$ and the actual confusion-derived mixing matrix $\hat{A}$ (using Ground Truth for analysis) at each step.
2.  Record the condition number of $A$ and the mass of the "sink column."

**Pass/Fail Criteria:**
* **Pass:** The $R^2$ remains high even with constraints, and $A$ significantly matches the actual confusion mixing matrix $\hat{A}$.
* **Fail:** Applying the constraints breaks the fit (low $R^2$), or $A$ cannot be interpreted as a confusion mixture.

**Interpretation:**
* **ACCEPT (H23a):** Confirms the "pseudo prototype is a confusion-mixed true prototype" model. This mathematically bridges the sink class, sample filtering, and debiasing, providing a direct key for the algorithm design.
* **REJECT (H23b):** Weakens the interpretation of H8. It suggests that the low-dimensional representation collapse (H20) dominates everything, shifting the design focus heavily toward geometric restoration.

### H24: Text Hubness Origin of the "Sink Class"

**Hypothesis (H24):**
The sink class (e.g., the "cat black hole") is not solely a visual-side artifact. It is driven by the text prototype $z_c$ acting as a geometric "hub" (having high average similarity with all other classes) in the text embedding space, or it is a bias introduced by the prompt selection.

**Measurement (Label-free, straightforward analysis):**
1.  Compute the hubness score using only text embeddings:
    $$h_c = \frac{1}{C-1} \sum_{l \ne c} \cos(z_c, z_l)$$
    (Alternatively, measure how often a class appears as a neighbor in text kNN).
2.  Calculate the correlation between each class's sink ratio (where OC-wrong samples flock) and its text hubness score $h_c$.
3.  Repeat the measurement across different prompt templates. (BATCLIP reports performance variance across templates).

**Pass/Fail Criteria:**
* **Pass:** The sink frequency heavily depends on text hubness $h_c$ and changes with prompt templates.
* **Fail:** Text hubness shows no correlation, and the sink class remains identical on the visual side regardless of text embeddings.

**Interpretation:**
* **ACCEPT:** The design focus shifts beyond visual sample filtering to logit adjustment, text-side de-hubbing, prompt adaptation, or text normalization.
* **REJECT:** The sink is primarily a consequence of visual collapse. The core solution must focus on geometric restoration (H20/H26).

### H25: Quantifying "Harmful Features over Wrong Labels" via Per-Sample Gradient Influence

**Background:**
This resolves the ambiguity in H9 regarding why "Oracle-correct" fails.

**Hypothesis (H25):**
High-margin wrong samples (especially those pulled into the sink class) are high-influence outliers that yield disproportionately large per-sample gradient norms for the PM/SP losses, severely distorting the prototype direction. Therefore, merely "correcting the label" remains harmful because their corrupted features still inject massive, noisy gradients.

**Measurement:**
1.  Compute the per-sample gradient norm (or an influence proxy) for each sample $i$:
    $$\|\nabla_{\phi}(\ell_{pm,i})\|, \quad \|\nabla_{\phi}(\ell_{sp,i})\|$$
2.  Regress/correlate these norms against $s_{max}$, kNN agreement, and aug-consistency:
    * Do wrong & low-$s_{max}$ samples have larger gradients?
    * Do samples predicted as the sink class have larger gradients?

**Pass/Fail Criteria:**
* **Pass:** Wrong/low-evidence samples exhibit significantly larger gradients/influence.
* **Fail:** Wrong samples do not have exceptionally large gradients, suggesting the issue is "directional bias" (hubness/confusion) rather than "magnitude influence."

**Interpretation:**
* **ACCEPT:** Directly justifies optimization techniques robust to noisy gradients, such as Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), gradient clipping, or sample screening (typical in SoTTA/SAR approaches).

### H26: Is $d_{eff}$ Recovery a Causal Lever or Just a Prerequisite? (Label-free Geometric Intervention)

**Background:**
H20 shows $d_{eff}$ and $Var_{inter}$ act as identical state variables, but the correlation between $d_{eff}$ and accuracy is only moderate (0.364). This implies we must recover "useful dimensions." We need to verify if artificially intervening to raise $d_{eff}$ directly improves accuracy.

**Hypothesis (H26):**
Applying batch-level feature whitening/decorrelation (label-free) to artificially raise $d_{eff}$ will yield a meaningful accuracy improvement, especially in the 1K (5-step) regime.

**Measurement (Intervention; feasible at test-time):**
1.  Apply a "batch covariance normalization" (e.g., ZCA whitening) to the features independently of the LN updates, or add a whitening loss.
2.  Measure:
    * The delta in $d_{eff}$
    * The delta in accuracy
    * The change in the sink ratio
    *(Note: Recent research explicitly claims TTA stabilization/generalization via whitening, e.g., Dual-Phase Whitening).*

**Pass/Fail Criteria:**
* **Pass:** An induced increase in $d_{eff}$ causally leads to an increase in accuracy (especially at 5 steps).
* **Fail:** $d_{eff}$ increases but accuracy stagnates, confirming that "directional alignment" (text-aligned recovery) is the decisive factor, not just overall volume.

### H27: The Importance of Direction: Does Text-Span Projected $d_{eff}$ Better Predict Accuracy?

**Hypothesis (H27):**
The effective dimensionality and variance within the specific subspace spanned by the text prototypes (the dimensions that directly contribute to the logits) will predict accuracy much better than the global $d_{eff}$. (i.e., The dimensions that "wake up" must be classification-relevant).

**Measurement:**
1.  Define $P_Z$ as the projection onto the subspace spanned by the text embeddings.
2.  Decompose the feature vector: $v = v_\parallel + v_\perp$ (where $v_\parallel = P_Z v$).
3.  Compare the correlations: $\rho(d_{eff}(v_\parallel), \text{Acc})$ vs. $\rho(d_{eff}(v), \text{Acc})$, and similarly for $Var_{inter}$.

**Pass/Fail Criteria:**
* **Pass:** Projected metrics significantly outperform global metrics in predicting accuracy $\rightarrow$ "Text-aligned geometric restoration" is the key.
* **Fail:** Both metrics perform similarly $\rightarrow$ General global isotropy restoration is sufficient.

### H28: Disentangling the 10K Advantage — "Steps" vs. "Fresh Data"

**Background:**
H10 confirms optimization dynamics matter, but it does not disentangle whether the gain comes from taking "more steps" or seeing "more novel samples." This is critical for solving the 1K regime problem.

**Hypothesis (H28):**
The performance gain in the 10K regime stems primarily from executing "more gradient steps" (even if reusing data) rather than from encountering "fresh/novel samples."

**Measurement (Ablation Study):**
* **Setting A:** Recirculate 1K data to run for 50 steps (maintains online batch size, but artificially repeats data for diagnostics).
* **Setting B:** Use 10K data but stop updating after exactly 5 steps.
* **Setting C:** Standard 10K sequential run (50 steps over 10K unique samples).

**Pass/Fail Criteria & Interpretation:**
* **If A approaches C:** The core driver is the *number of steps / optimization dynamics*. $\rightarrow$ The design key for the 1K regime is "few-step acceleration" (e.g., larger learning rates, aggressive momentum, or different optimizers).
* **If A is low and only C is high:** The core driver is *fresh data diversity*. $\rightarrow$ The design key is "memory / caching / cumulative statistics" to artificially expand the effective batch diversity in short streams.
